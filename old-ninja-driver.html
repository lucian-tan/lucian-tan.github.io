Unlocking new courier transactions

Enhancing an Android digital manifest app for Southeast Asian couriers

Overview

The couriers at Ninja Van, a last-mile logistics company, use an Android application to obtain and complete their daily delivery tasks, and limitations within the original application were causing operational issues for the business. Through collaborations with various teams, the app was re-designed to eliminate erroneous workflows and accommodate more operational procedures. This resulted in fewer operational issues, faster re-training, and a better foundation for future business requirements.

Scope

User experience design, user interface design

Details

Internal Android product
2016

Background

Reaching a new market segment

Ninja Van, a B2B last-mile logistics company in Southeast Asia, wanted to introduce a B2C on-demand pick-up & delivery service in Jakarta to reach new segments in the social commerce market and establish a stronger brand presence in Indonesia.

After the initial plan to integrate social media platforms with an order creation feature fell apart, the company quickly shifted the focus to on-demand logistics services for smaller-scale e-commerce sellers. When the online application launched in 2017 to a tepid response, my team was assigned to improve the user experience of this product.

What it was originally

The original concept was a logistics and cash payment service for small, e-commerce business owners to sell and deliver to their customers via their social media platforms. When the plan for a social platform listing feature fell apart, that feature was removed from the released product.

The feature set of the application includes:

  • Requesting for parcel pick up at their desired location
  • Checking delivery prices on the application
  • Tracking progress of ongoing deliveries
  • E-wallet management

Problem

Low user acquisition, low conversions

In the first few months after the publicised launch of the service, the adoption rate was poor with a high drop-off rate. Observing the usage analytics, we noticed that the drop-off happens mainly at these points:

  • On the page where the user sets their pick up location
  • On the page where the user enters the parcel details
  • On the dashboard after the user completes entering parcel information, but chooses not to request for a courier

Process

Discover, learn, iterate

For this consumer service, we studied usage analytics to find areas of opportunity, and conducted user research when necessary to find the reasons behind the problems. The operations team is also involved in the design process to provide expertise on the possible procedures and processes.

Research

Target users

In our target market of Jakarta city, Indonesia, the key users are:

Home business owners

They use e-commerce to sell their goods from their homes or personal spaces, and usually have to pack and make logistics transactions on their own.

Small retail businesses

Though they own retail spaces, they also sell via e-commerce channels. They have to manage on-site and online sales with few staff members.

Hearing from the users

The product manager, Antoni (who speaks Indonesian), conducted qualitative research with the Indonesian team in Jakarta city, meeting small business owners to learn how they use social commerce.

The Indonesian team also recruited home business owners to conduct a focus group session, so as to understand their processes better, and to discover how they interact with the current application.

Areas for improvement

The participants were presented with the application for the first time and tried out the main ‘try-out’ flow. From their experiences, we found the following insights.

“This isn’t my place!”

When participants were setting up their pickup location, they were confused to receive incorrect place names on the map. This problem with the map API (due to coverage issues in Indonesia) also made it difficult for them to search for their locations accurately. We learnt that this issue was leaving poor impressions at the start of their experience.

“Why does it take so long to know the cost?”

Knowing the cost of delivery is a priority for the marketplace research participants. Participants took ~2 minutes to complete entering one parcel's details on the app, and they considered that too slow and cumbersome to obtain the prices.

“This could be quite tedious.”

Participants noted that entering the parcel details could be very time-consuming for larger quantities of parcels. Some participants stated that if they have to go through the same process in the application for more parcels, they would use a different service.

“I’m not ready to request, but is everything saved?”

Going through the try-out flow, some participants were unwilling to click on the ‘request pick up’ button when they were not ready to execute that action. They expressed desire to continue the process later on, and wanted to know that their inputs were saved.

Objective

A faster, more intuitive experience

This service was conceived to make dealing with logistics less cumbersome, so the goal is to improve the user experience of the application so that users spend less time going through the steps, or figuring out how, to call for logistics services to their doorstep.

The areas we identified are:

  1. Matching the experience to user expectations
  2. Resolving location setting issues
  3. Show parcel fees faster
  4. Reducing the steps to a pick up request

Process changes

Collaborating with operations

To make some of our ideas work, we needed to tap on the operational capabilities of the company. The product team discussed with the Indonesian operations team to find out what we could introduce without affecting other operating procedures adversely.

“How might we reduce the number of steps/inputs to make a pickup request?”

As this product has much lower volumes than the core routine operations, we were able to devise a new process that enables a less taxing user experience on the application. By having our operational staff digitise the parcel information on behalf of the customer, we could unlock different user flows that deliver a more convenient experience without complicating our existing procedures.

Updating the rest of the chain

All these modifications to the application’s experience affected the operations on the ground, hence the internal applications that our various operators use to carry out their tasks are updated for them to handle the new tasks and edge cases of this service. These added functions are necessary to execute cash collection, digitise data, and change details effectively.

Application outcomes

Better flows for new and returning customers

With the operational update, we improved the user interface for a more effortless try-out and repeat use flow:

Registration at the right point

To assure new customers that their inputs are saved during the try-out flow, we changed the sequence so that customers will register for an account right after adding their first parcel, removing the gap that occurs when users hesitate to press the call-to-action button.

Confirming locations gradually

We changed the location process to prevent errors for first-time customers, and to cater to user expectations better.

  1. We modified the process such that new customers only confirm their district level information at the start, so that the pricing engine can run and customers can correct wrong address suggestions swiftly.
  1. After obtaining the prices for their parcels and ready to call for a courier, customers can provide the full address details for their pick up, and are able to edit their GPS location (if incorrect). We also saved previously-used addresses for added convenience.
  1. The addresses that customers used for successful pickups are saved, so they can use them for their next pickup easily.

"It takes so many steps to know the fees!"

Greater visibility, clearer response

While we considered alternative touchpoints to make the fees more discoverable, we decided that it was best to keep it within the application so as to direct traffic to the application. We realised that the layout of the form was making participants enter more details than required to receive the information they need, extending their interaction time unnecessarily. We re-arranged the form inputs, so that customers can clearly see updated prices with every input change, and decide if they want to continue.

“This feels tedious.”

No more repetition

With the new operational process, we no longer require customers to enter label information that they already have to attach to the parcel digitally. This shortens the time spent on entering parcel details significantly. Customers that prefer to enter the information themselves still have the option to do so.

Skip it all & just call

To encourage return customers to send higher quantities of parcels, we added the option to skip the process of entering individual parcel details. By indicating the type and quantity of parcels they want to send, customers can request for a pick up (with the right vehicle type assigned) with less hassle.

Reservations for later

Following the customer preference to request for a courier later from our research, a scheduling feature was added to give customers flexibility to secure bookings for a later time.

Results

Faster and more completions

After the design updates were released, we noticed a small uptick to the number of requests, though the weekly numbers were still low. A good indicator was that new users spent less screen time at each screen before progressing to the next. The increased traffic to the website and application could have been influenced by other factors, such as more effective marketing campaigns later on.

Lessons

Consider both the foreground and the background

We would not be able to introduce a dramatic change like enabling no-input parcel pick up, if not for the Indonesian operating team collaborating to make it possible operationally. While the initial cost of this change was high, the investment paid off by enabling other new products and services.

Don't make assumptions of user behaviour

The discovery of what our target users in Indonesia expected in the sign-up process was an eye-opener for our team. It was a good reminder that our beliefs and expectations are not universal, and that extra effort should be taken to fully understand the target users when creating a product.

Organise UX writing well for translations

Building a product in one language with plans to release it in another requires more care to ensure that the intended messaging is not lost in translation. We used a spreadsheet to consolidate all the descriptions and labels, serving as a ‘source of truth’ for our collaborations with other departments, and to maintain a consistent tone throughout the user experience.
